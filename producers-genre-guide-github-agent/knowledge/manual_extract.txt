PART I — Foundations Every Producer Needs
1. The Producer Mindset & Workflow
A music producer is not only someone who “makes beats.” In professional terms, the producer is responsible for the
overall sonic identity, arrangement decisions, performance direction, and the emotional arc of a record. In bedroom
and DAW-native workflows, you often wear producer, songwriter, engineer, and mixer hats at once. Genre literacy is
what lets you choose the right decisions under pressure — not rigid rules, but recognizable contracts with the
listener’s ear.
Every genre answers a different set of questions for the audience: How hard should the low end hit? How much
space should drums leave for vocals? Is the groove behind, on, or ahead of the beat? Is harmony dense or sparse?
Is the mix polished and competitive, or intentionally raw? When you understand those contracts, you can either fulfill
them cleanly or break them deliberately (which is how new micro-genres form).
A reliable start-to-finish workflow
• Reference first: Import 2–4 commercial tracks in the target genre. Match rough loudness, study drum density,
vocal placement, and sidechain behavior.
• Tempo & grid: Set BPM and decide swing/groove template before writing. Wrong grid = wrong feel forever.
• Core loop: Build a 4–8 bar idea that already feels like the genre (drums + bass + one harmonic/melodic hook).
• Expand arrangement: Map intro / verse / pre / chorus / bridge / outro (or dance form: intro–build–drop–break).
• Sound design pass: Replace placeholders with final-quality samples/synths. Layer intentionally.
• Vocal / lead focus: If there is a vocal, everything else serves it. Carve mix space early.
• Mix in context: Balance, EQ, compression, spatial effects, automation while arrangement is still flexible.
• Print & rest: Export, walk away, then revise with fresh ears against references.
Producer tip: Finish more tracks than you perfect. Genre fluency comes from completing 20 average songs in a style,
not one endlessly tweaked loop.
2. Core Music Theory for Production
You do not need conservatory training to produce commercially, but you do need practical theory: keys, scales,
intervals, chords, voice leading, and how rhythm interacts with harmony. Theory is a map of options — it speeds up
decisions and helps you communicate with musicians.
Keys, modes, and genre tendencies
Minor keys dominate modern hip-hop, trap, drill, metal, and much electronic music because they read as serious,
dark, or emotional. Major keys still own large parts of pop, country, gospel, and uplifting dance. Modes matter: Dorian
(minor with raised 6th) feels groovy/soulful; Phrygian (flat 2) feels Spanish/metal/dark electronic; Mixolydian (major
with flat 7) feels bluesy/rock; Lydian (raised 4) feels dreamy/cinematic. Aeolian (natural minor) is the default
“sad/modern” palette. Harmonic minor (raised 7th) creates tension used in metal, classical, and some EDM.
Chord vocabulary that actually gets used
• Triads: major, minor, diminished, augmented — foundation of almost everything.
• 7ths: maj7, m7, dominant 7 — essential for R&B, jazz, neo-soul, lo-fi, gospel.
• Extensions: 9, 11, 13 — add color without changing function; stack carefully to avoid mud.
• Sus2 / sus4: open, modern, pop/ambient friendly.
• Power chords (root + 5th): rock/metal guitars; leave third out for distortion clarity.
• Parallel minor/major moves: common emotional “lift” or “drop” in pop choruses.

• Modal interchange: borrow bVI, bVII, iv from parallel minor into major progressions (very pop/rock).
Voice leading & bass motion
Listeners hear bass motion and top-line melody more clearly than inner chord notes. Strong bass lines often move by
step or by fifths. Keep common tones when changing chords; move other voices the shortest distance. In electronic
genres, the “bass” may be a mono synth following root notes with slides (portamento) rather than walking jazz lines
— both are valid, genre-dependent choices.
Rhythm theory producers actually use
Meter is usually 4/4 in commercial music; 3/4 and 6/8 appear in ballads, waltzes, and some folk/country; odd meters
(5/4, 7/8) appear in progressive and experimental work. Subdivision (8ths, 16ths, triplets) defines genre feel as much
as tempo. Swing ratios (straight vs swung 16ths) separate house from boom bap from jazz. Syncopation — accents
off the strong beats — is the soul of funk, Latin, Afrobeats, and modern R&B.
3. Sound Design Basics (Synths, Samples, FX)
Sound design is choosing and shaping timbre so that the arrangement reads as a coherent world. Genres have
timbral signatures: 808s and dark pads for trap; supersaws for trance; Rhodes and vinyl crackle for lo-fi; tight acoustic
kits for jazz; distorted guitars for metal. Learn subtractive synthesis first (oscillators → filter → amp envelopes →
modulation), then FM, wavetable, granular, and sampling.
The signal chain mental model
• Source: oscillator, sample, recording, noise generator.
• Tone shape: filter (LP/HP/BP/notch), waveshaping, distortion, bitcrush.
• Dynamics shape: ADSR envelopes on amp and filter; compression later in mix.
• Motion: LFOs, step sequencers, macros, automation.
• Space: delay, reverb, chorus, phaser, stereo width tools.
• Glue: bus compression, saturation, clipping for loudness and character.
Sampling ethics & technique
Classic hip-hop and many electronic styles are sample-based. Legally, commercial releases usually require
clearance or use royalty-free / original material. Creatively: chop to transient, pitch to key, time-stretch with algorithms
that preserve (or intentionally destroy) transients, layer with one-shots, and process heavily so the sample becomes a
new instrument. Resampling your own synths is a pro habit — print audio, then treat it as a sample for further
mangling.
4. Drums, Groove & Rhythm Foundations
If harmony is the body of a track, drums are the heartbeat. Genre is often recognized in the first bar by kick pattern,
snare/clap placement, hi-hat density, and percussion vocabulary.
The four drum pillars
• Kick: weight, length, click (beater/transient), mono low end. Tune kick to key when possible.
• Snare/clap: backbeat identity. Layer body + snap + room/verb tail. Ghost notes add human feel.
• Hats/cymbals: timekeeper and energy. Open hat on offbeats is classic house; 1/32 rolls are trap.
• Percussion: shakers, congas, rims, snaps, foley — they create culture-specific identity.
Humanization without chaos
Quantize to a groove template rather than hard 100% grid for swing genres. Vary velocities (especially hats). Nudge
selected hits 5–15 ms early/late for push/pull. Use slightly different samples on repeats (round-robin).

5. Arrangement Architecture
Arrangement is storytelling with energy. Song-based genres (pop, rock, R&B, country) usually follow
verse–pre–chorus–chorus structures with a bridge. Dance genres use DJ-friendly structures with long intros for
mixing, tension builds, and drops. Hip-hop often loops with section changes marked by vocal density, ad-libs, and
instrumental drops rather than huge harmonic shifts.
Energy tools (any genre)
• Add/remove layers (filter open = energy up; filter closed = energy down).
• Change drum density (half-time vs double-time feels).
• Rise/fall FX, reverse cymbals, white noise sweeps, impact hits.
• Silence / dropouts (1 bar of near-silence before a drop is classic).
• Vocal chops as transitions; risers into choruses.
• Key lift (modulation up a semitone/whole tone) for final chorus — use sparingly.
• Breakdown: strip to pads/vocals, then rebuild.
6. Mixing & Mastering Essentials by Genre Context
Mixing goals change by genre. Pop/R&B wants vocal intelligibility and polish. Techno wants club translation and
mono-compatible low end. Metal wants guitar clarity and drum impact without fizz. Lo-fi may intentionally limit
bandwidth and add noise. Always mix on multiple systems (monitors, headphones, phone, car) and check mono.
Universal mix order of operations
• Gain staging: peaks healthy, headroom for processing (−6 to −12 dBFS peaks early).
• High-pass non-bass elements carefully; leave room for kick/bass relationship.
• Balance faders first — if it doesn’t work at static balance, plugins won’t save it.
• EQ subtractively to fix; additively for character.
• Compression for control and groove (bus glue vs peak control vs parallel punch).
• Spatial effects last in creative chain; filter delays/verbs so they don’t muddy lows.
• Automation is mix magic: rides on vocals, filter moves, send levels by section.
• Mastering: gentle EQ, multiband control if needed, limiter for competitive loudness. Leave true peak headroom
(~−1 dBTP streaming).
Producer tip: The kick–bass relationship is the #1 genre-defining mix problem. Solve it with complementary EQ,
sidechain, or frequency splitting (kick owns sub on hits; bass fills between) before adding more low-end layers.

7. Boom Bap & Classic Hip-Hop
The head-nod foundation of golden-era hip-hop production
Typical BPM
85–98 (sweet spot ~90–94)
Feel
Swung or lightly swung 16ths; hard kick/snare “boom–bap”
Keys
Minor common; samples often dictate key/mode
Core elements
Sampled drums or SP-style kits, chopped loops, bass, scratches, raw vocals
Mix vibe
Mid-forward, punchy drums, not ultra-loud modern streaming smash
Historical DNA
Boom bap (named for kick “boom” and snare “bap”) crystallized in late-80s/90s New York — Think DJ Premier, Pete
Rock, Large Professor, RZA, Q-Tip as producers who defined the vocabulary. The aesthetic values crate-digging,
imperfect loops, dusty texture, and drums that feel like they hit your chest without modern 808 sub dominance.
Drum programming
Kick & snare
• Kick on 1 and often the “and” of 2 or other syncopations; not always four-on-the-floor.
• Snare on 2 and 4 with authority — this is the “bap.” Layer a tight snare + roomy snare for body.
• Ghost snares and rimshots between hits create pocket (especially Premier-style).
Hats & percussion
• Closed hats in 8ths or swung 16ths; occasional open hat.
• Perc: bongos, shakers, vinyl noise, chopped break fragments.
Classic approach
• Start from breakbeats (Amen, Funky Drummer–style vocabulary historically) or recreate with one-shots.
• Use MPC-style timing: slight swing, velocity variation, sometimes non-quantized chops.
Sampling & harmony
Chop soul, jazz, funk records into 1–4 bar loops or micro-chops rearranged into new progressions. Pitch samples to a
key; filter mud; add saturation. Common harmony: minor 7 loops, ii–V suggestions, modal vamps. Bass can be
sampled electric bass or a simple sine/triangle following roots. Leave space for vocals — boom bap mixes are often
less dense than modern trap.
Arrangement
• Intro: filtered loop or scratch phrase.
• Verse: full drums + loop; occasional dropouts for punchlines.
• Hook: add chorus vocals, counter-melody, or horn stab; sometimes switch chop.
• Bridge/breakdown: drums drop or switch to different sample.
• Outro: scratches, fade, or beat stop.
Producer tip: Print your sample through tape/console emulation, then re-chop. Character compounds.

Mixing notes
• Drums slightly louder than modern pop relative to music bed.
• High-pass samples around 100–200 Hz if bass is separate; or let sample carry lows carefully.
• Parallel compression on drums for punch.
• Vocal: presence 2–5 kHz, de-ess, short plate or room; keep intelligible over dusty beds.
• Avoid over-limiting; preserve transient snap of the snare.

8. Trap & Modern Hip-Hop
808s, hi-hat sewing machines, dark melodies, streaming-era low end
Typical BPM
130–160 with half-time feel (feels like 65–80) OR 70–90 straight
Feel
Half-time snare on 3; rapid hat rolls; sliding 808s
Keys
Minor (natural, harmonic, Phrygian); dark modes
Core elements
808 bass, crisp snare/clap, hi-hat rolls, dark bells/pads/leads, ad-libs
Mix vibe
Loud, sub-heavy, vocal-forward, wide tops, mono lows
What “trap” means for producers
Trap evolved from Southern hip-hop (Atlanta lineage especially) into a global production language. Even non-rap
genres now borrow trap drums. The signature is the relationship between a long, tuned 808 bass drum and intricate
hi-hat programming, usually with a snare/clap emphasizing beat 3 in a half-time grid at higher BPMs.
Drum blueprint
Grid & snare
• Set 140 BPM as a common starting point. Place snare/clap on beat 3 of each bar (half-time).
• Layer clap + snare + maybe a transient click. Short reverb (0.6–1.2s) with high-pass on the verb.
Hi-hats
• 1/8 and 1/16 patterns as base; add 1/32 and 1/64 rolls into downbeats.
• Pitch automation on rolls (rising or falling) is a genre cliché that still works when musical.
• Velocity: not flat — humanize lightly or program accents.
• Open hats and rides for section lift.
Kick vs 808
• Often the 808 IS the kick (long sine with punch). Sometimes a short kick layers on top of 808 hits.
• Program 808 pattern as a bassline: roots, fifths, chromatic slides, dead notes (short muted hits).
• Portamento/glide on monophonic 808 is essential for modern trap melodies in the bass.
Melody & sound design
• Dark plucks, bells, Rhodes, distorted pianos, flute/voice-like leads, minor key arps.
• Keep motifs simple and loopable; hip-hop thrives on hypnotic repetition with variation.
• Scale choices: natural minor, harmonic minor for Middle-Eastern spice, Phrygian for menace.
• Counter-melodies enter in hooks; verses may be sparser.
• Atmos: reverse FX, distant choirs, risers, vinyl optional (less than lo-fi).
Arrangement map (typical song)
• 0:00 Intro (melodic motif + FX, drums enter partially)
• 0:15 Verse 1 (full drums, vocal main)
• 0:45 Pre/Hook setup (hat energy up, riser)

• 1:00 Chorus/Hook (fullest layers, ad-libs)
• 1:30 Verse 2 (variation: new 808 line or melody layer)
• 2:00 Hook 2
• 2:30 Bridge/breakdown or beat switch
• 3:00 Final hook + outro
Producer tip: Program the 808 and vocal melody to leave pocket for each other — if both fight mid-low, cut 200–400

Mixing trap specifically
• 808: mono below ~120 Hz; saturation for small-speaker audibility; multiband or dynamic EQ vs kick click.
• Sidechain hats/pads lightly to 808 if needed; classic is ducking pads/music to kick/808.
• Vocal chain: tuning (subtle or hard), de-ess, compression (serial), presence EQ, delay throws, short verb,
autopan ad-libs.
• Stereo: keep sub mono; widen bells/pads/FX; check phase.
• Loudness: competitive but leave headroom for streaming codecs; true peak limit.

9. Drill (UK & US)
Sliding 808s, menacing patterns, street narrative energy
Typical BPM
UK: ~138–146 | US Chicago-rooted: ~60–70 half-time feel / ~130–145
Feel
Stuttering hats, sharp snares, sliding 808 basslines, dark minor melodies
Keys
Minor, often very dark; sparse harmony
Core elements
808 slides, cold piano/lead, aggressive vocal delivery, drill snare patterns
Mix vibe
Hard-hitting, cold, vocal center, ominous space
UK vs US distinctions
Chicago drill (Chief Keef era influence) emphasized bleak melodies and a distinctive drum bounce. UK drill evolved
with different rhythmic cadences, more complex 808 slides, and a colder sonic palette that influenced global drill (NY
drill, etc.). As a producer, study regional drum patterns and vocal flows separately — the 808 writing is a major
differentiator.
Production focus
• 808s with long glides between notes; chromatic approach notes; silence as part of the pattern.
• Snares often sharper and more syncopated than generic trap.
• Melodies: minor piano loops, tense strings, desaturated bells — avoid happy major progressions.
• Leave rhythmic space for aggressive vocal syncopation.
• Sound selection is colder: less “musical theater,” more “street night air.”
Producer tip: Write the 808 after a rough vocal rhythm sketch when possible — drill 808s converse with flow.

10. R&B / Contemporary R&B
Groove, harmony color, vocal intimacy, sensual low end
Typical BPM
60–90 ballad/groove | 90–115 midtempo | some uptempo 115–130
Feel
Swung or deeply pocketed; ghost notes; syncopated bass
Keys
Rich harmony: 7ths, 9ths, 11ths; major & minor; modal interchange
Core elements
Electric bass or 808 hybrid, keys (Rhodes/piano), guitars, airy drums, stacked vocals
Mix vibe
Vocal throne, warm mids, smooth top, controlled sub
Harmonic language
Contemporary R&B lives in extended chords. Learn progressions using maj7, m9, dom9, half-diminished, and slash
chords. Voice lead smoothly. Neo-soul (D’Angelo, Erykah Badu lineage) pushes jazz harmony further; mainstream
R&B may simplify for hooks but still colors verses. Borrow chords from parallel modes freely. Use secondary
dominants to approach the iv or V with tension.
Drum & groove
• Kick patterns syncopate around the vocal; not always simple 1-and-3.
• Snare/clap can be layered softly; rimshots and snaps for verses.
• Hats: swung 16ths, shakers layered, sparse opens.
• Program ghost kicks and ghost snares for pocket.
• Live-feeling kits or crisp modern one-shots both work depending on substyle (throwback vs dark R&B).
Bass
Electric bass (DI + amp sim) with slides, dead notes, and syncopation is classic. Modern R&B often blends 808 sub
under melodic bass. Sidechain gently to kick. Follow chord tones: roots, fifths, approach chromatics, occasional thirds
for sweetness.
Vocals & arrangement
• Lead intimate and dry-ish in verses; wetter wider choruses.
• Stacks: thirds/fifths harmonies, octaves, whispered doubles, ad-lib answers.
• Pre-chorus lifts via rising chords, drum fills, or filter opens.
• Breakdowns with pads and voice are emotional peaks.
Producer tip: Automate a “closer mic” feel in verses (less reverb, more proximity/bass on vocal) and open the space in
choruses.
Sound palette
Rhodes, Wurlitzer, soft acoustic piano, clean/chorus guitars, atmospheric pads, vinyl optional, subtle tape wow. Avoid
harsh supersaws unless doing hybrid pop-R&B. Distortion is satin, not metal.

11. Soul, Neo-Soul & Gospel
Human feel, choir power, organ/piano truth, emotional dynamics
Typical BPM
70–100 soul/gospel ballads to midgroove; uptempo gospel can push higher
Feel
Behind-the-beat pocket; swing; dynamic human performance
Keys
Gospel: rich progressions, secondary dominants, “shout” sections; soul: classic I–IV–V variants + soul
cadences
Core elements
Piano/organ, choir or stacked BGVs, live drums or realistic kits, bass guitar, tambourine
Mix vibe
Warm, wide vocals, natural rooms, dynamic (not over-squashed)
Gospel production essentials
• Hammond organ (B3) with Leslie simulation is iconic; piano comps with rhythmic stabs.
• Chord vocabulary includes walk-ups, passing diminished chords, and big dominant chains.
• Choir arrangement: SATB thinking even with stacked solo singers — write parts that harmonize the melody.
• Tambourine on offbeats/upbeats in celebratory sections.
• Dynamic form: quiet testimony → building → full praise peak.
Neo-soul notes
Imperfect timing as aesthetic, Rhodes-centered harmony, muffled drums, intimate vocals, jazz-informed chords, and
arrangements that feel like a band in a small room. Resist over-quantizing. Saturation and gentle compression glue
the band bus.

12. Funk & Disco
The almighty pocket: syncopation, four-on-the-floor variants, bass as lead
Typical BPM
Funk: 90–115 | Disco: 110–130 (classic ~118–124)
Feel
16th-note syncopation; guitar chicken scratch; interlocking parts
Keys
Dominant 7, Mixolydian vamps, minor funk vamps, disco major joy
Core elements
Bass guitar heroics, tight drums, rhythm guitar, horns, strings (disco), percussion
Mix vibe
Punchy, danceable, clear midrange rhythm, strong mono kick/bass
Funk drum & bass
Funk drums emphasize the “one” (James Brown doctrine) while snare and hats create conversation. Ghost notes are
mandatory. Bass is melodic and rhythmic — ghost notes, slides, 16th syncopations. Guitar plays muted 16ths (wah
optional). Horns hit stabs on offbeats. Arrange as interlocking puzzle pieces; each part leaves space for others
(negative space is funk).
Disco blueprint
• Four-on-the-floor kick with open hat on offbeats (classic).
• Octave bass lines (root–octave patterns) very common.
• Lush strings, rhythm guitars, piano four-to-the-floor comps or offbeat comps.
• Build for the dancefloor: long intros, percussion breaks, filter fun.
• Modern nu-disco adds sidechain pumping and cleaner digital polish.
Producer tip: Record or program guitar and bass with slight timing push/pull — perfect quantize kills disco sweat.

PART III — Electronic Dance Music
EDM umbrella genres prioritize the club/festival body response: kick energy, hypnotic repetition, DJ-friendly
arrangement, and sound design as the “lead singer.” Subgenres differ wildly in tempo, drum patterns, bass design,
and harmonic density. Always test on a system with real sub if you produce for clubs.

13. House (Deep, Tech, Progressive, Future)
Four-on-the-floor soul of the dancefloor
Typical BPM
118–130 (classic house ~120–125; tech house often 124–128)
Feel
Four-on-the-floor kick; offbeat open hats; groovy basslines
Keys
Minor house common; deep house jazzier; future house brighter pop harmony
Core elements
Kick, clap/snare on 2/4, hats, bass, chords/stabs, vocal chops or full vocals
Mix vibe
Club-ready low end, wide percussion, clear mid hooks
Universal house drum grid
• Kick on every quarter note (1, 2, 3, 4).
• Clap/snare on 2 and 4.
• Closed hats 8th or 16th; open hat on offbeats (the “and” of each beat) is classic.
• Percussion layers (shakers, congas, bongos, rim) create swing and identity.
• Groove templates: slight swing on 16ths for classic house feel.
Substyle cheat sheet
• Deep house: warmer chords (7/9), Rhodes/pads, soulful vocals, smoother bass, lush but not harsh.
• Tech house: minimal melodic content, hypnotic percussion, stripped bass, DJ tool structures.
• Progressive house: longer builds, emotional breakdowns, bigger chord stacks, festival-friendly.
• Future house: heavy sidechain, rubbery bass, bright plucks, pop structures, modern sheen.
• Afro house: organic percussion, log drums, tribal rhythms, spiritual/atmospheric pads.
Bass & sound design
Bass often plays syncopated patterns against the kick. Sidechain compression (or volume ducking) to the kick is
nearly mandatory in modern house. Chords: short stabs or long filtered pads. Vocal chops as instruments are a
house staple — pitch them musically, rhythmically gate them, reverb/delay for width.
Arrangement for DJs
• 16–32 bar intro (percussion + kick) for mixing in.
• Groove establish → add bass → add hook → breakdown → build → drop (full elements).
• Mid-song break with filter automation.
• 16–32 bar outro for mixing out.
Producer tip: Mark phrase lengths in 8/16/32 bars. DJs and dancers feel these blocks unconsciously.

14. Techno
Hypnosis, functional design, industrial beauty, relentless pulse
Typical BPM
125–140 (peak-time often 130–135; slower deep techno exists)
Feel
Four-on-the-floor; machine precision; evolving textures over long forms
Keys
Often minor/modal; sometimes atonal/textural rather than chord-driven
Core elements
Powerful kick, hi-hats, metallic percussion, drones, acid lines, sparse motifs
Mix vibe
Mono-compatible kick, controlled mids, club translation over earbuds alone
Philosophy
Techno (Detroit roots; Berlin evolution) is less about hooks-per-minute and more about altered state through
repetition and gradual change. Production quality of the kick and the interest of the evolving layers matter more than
chord complexity.
How to build a techno track
• Design a kick that can carry 6+ minutes (layer sub + punch + click carefully).
• Add a complementary bass pulse or offbeat bass.
• Introduce metallic hats/rides; automate filters slowly.
• Use one memorable motif (stab, acid 303-style line, vocal fragment) sparingly.
• Arrangement: long arcs, subtractive changes, DJ-friendly structure.
• Sound design: industrial samples, FM metallic tones, noise beds, modular-style modulation.
Producer tip: Automate one parameter per long section (filter cutoff, reverb send, delay feedback) — techno is the art
of patient change.

15. Trance & Progressive Trance
Emotional supersaws, tension/release, uplifting journeys
Typical BPM
130–145 (uplifting often ~138; progressive can be lower)
Feel
Four-on-the-floor; rolling bass; big builds and euphoric releases
Keys
Minor for emotion; major lifts; dramatic chord progressions
Core elements
Supersaw leads, arps, pads, gated/chopped chords, snare rolls, uplifting leads
Mix vibe
Wide stereo leads, clean sub, shiny highs without ear fatigue
Sound design staples
• Supersaw: multiple detuned saws, unison, slight stereo spread, filter envelope for pluck/lead shapes.
• Arpeggiators locked to tempo; rhythmic gates on pads/chords.
• Snare rolls: increasing density into downbeat; sometimes pitch risers with them.
• Breakdown: strip drums, emotional melody/chords, then full rebuild into drop.
• Bass: rolling offbeat or on-beat patterns supporting kick; sidechain heavily for pumping if style fits.
Writing emotional progressions
Use longer chord durations in breakdowns, suspend resolutions, and introduce the main lead only when earned.
Counter-melodies in fifths/thirds stack for “big” feeling. Avoid overcrowding the lead’s midrange.

16. Drum & Bass / Jungle
Amen history, 170 BPM energy, sub bass science, break science
Typical BPM
170–178 (liquid can sit ~170; neurofunk similar range)
Feel
Fast breakbeats or programmed breaks; half-time bass feel often
Keys
Minor common; liquid more harmonic; neuro more sound-design lead
Core elements
Breaks/reprogrammed drums, sub bass, reese bass, pads (liquid), sound design (neuro)
Mix vibe
Sub clarity, mid bass aggression (neuro), airy tops, DJ-friendly drops
Breakbeat craft
Historically, jungle/DnB sliced funk breaks (Amen break famously). Modern production: use high-quality break
samples or synthesize with one-shots. Slice by transient, rearrange, layer with acoustic kick/snare for weight.
Ghosting and edits create the “rolling” feel. Parallel compression for glue and aggression.
Bass categories
• Sub bass: clean sine/triangle mono fundamental — the club floor mover.
• Reese: detuned saws with movement (LFOs, phasing) — mid-bass body.
• Neuro bass: complex FM/wavetable/modulation chains, multiband distortion, automation as performance.
• Relationship: sub carries weight; mid bass carries character. Split bands and process separately.
Substyles
• Liquid: musical chords, vocals, smoother drums, emotional pads.
• Jump-up: bouncy bass phrases, energetic, simpler hooks.
• Neurofunk: dark, technical sound design, precise drums, sci-fi atmospheres.
• Jungle revival: more break-centric, ragga samples, chaotic energy.
Producer tip: At 174 BPM, a 1-bar loop flies by — arrange in 8/16 bar phrases and use automation to avoid monotony.

17. Dubstep & Riddim
Wobbles, drops, half-time weight, sound design as the star
Typical BPM
138–150 with half-time feel (snare on 3)
Feel
Half-time groove; silence and space as weapons; massive drop contrast
Keys
Minor/dark; often riff-based rather than lush progressions
Core elements
Heavy kick/snare, growls/wobbles, reese, vocal chops, FX
Mix vibe
Huge perceived loudness on drop; clean sub; midrange aggression controlled
Drop writing
Dubstep is about contrast: sparse or atmospheric intros/verses explode into bass-weight drops. Design 2–4 unique
bass patches and sequence them as call-and-response. LFO rates on filters/wavetable positions synced to tempo
create wobbles (1/4, 1/8, 1/16 rates). Riddim emphasizes repetitive hypnotic bass motifs and hard-hitting minimal
drums.
Practical sound design tips
• Layer: sub (sine) + mid growl (distorted) + top click/noise for definition.
• Multiband distortion: distort mids hard, keep sub clean.
• Resample bass phrases to audio for further chopping and glitching.
• Transitions: silence, reverse bass, snare builds, vocal “hey” risers.
Producer tip: If the drop feels weak, the problem is often arrangement contrast or mid-bass clarity — not “needing
more plugins.”

18. UK Garage, 2-Step & Grime

Feel

Remove some kicks from a four-on-the-floor pattern so the kick “skips.” Syncopate snares/claps. Swing 16ths. Add

Grime instrumentals (“riddims”) leave huge space for MCs. Cold synths, simple dark loops, square/pulse leads,
sparse drums with distinctive bounce, and bold silence. Don’t overfill — the vocal is the lead instrument. Influence
from jungle/garage DNA remains in the energy.

21. Contemporary Pop
Hooks, contrast, vocal production, cross-genre borrowing
Typical BPM
90–130 depending on era trend; ballads slower; dance-pop higher
Feel
Clean quantized or lightly human; strong downbeats; chorus lift
Keys
Accessible progressions (I–V–vi–IV family and variants); key changes rare but effective
Core elements
Lead vocal, stacked BGVs, tight drums, bass, hook motif (synth/guitar)
Mix vibe
Vocal #1, competitive loudness, wide chorus, mono-compatible core
The pop arrangement contract
• Intro: short (4–8 bars) — get to vocal/hook fast for streaming.
• Verse: lower energy, storytelling, fewer layers.
• Pre-chorus: tension — rising melody, snare roll, filter open, chord lift.
• Chorus: highest energy, fullest stack, simplest/most memorable lyric-melody.
• Verse 2: slight variation (new counter-melody or drum fill pattern).
• Bridge: contrast (half-time, new chords, stripped or climactic).
• Final chorus: extra layer (harmony, ad-libs, key lift optional).
Vocal production (the real pop instrument)
• Comping: select best syllables from multiple takes.
• Tuning: transparent for most pop; stylistic hard tune when intentional.
• Timing: align doubles; keep lead human enough.
• Stacks: doubles, triples on chorus; octaves; harmonies on key words.
• FX: slap delay, 1/4 or 1/8 delay throws, short plates, creative transitions (reverse vocal).
• De-ess and control sibilance before brightening.
Writing with production in mind
Leave space in the beat for the chorus lyric rhythm. Use call-and-response between synth hook and vocal. Mute
instruments under important words. Automate interest so verse 2 never feels identical to verse 1.
Producer tip: If the chorus doesn’t feel bigger, remove verse elements rather than only adding chorus elements —
contrast is relative.

25. Rock & Indie Rock
Guitars as narrative, band dynamics, human performance
Typical BPM
100–160 depending on substyle; indie often midtempo
Feel
Live push/pull; backbeat snare; riff or chord-driven
Keys
Power chord rock; indie more diatonic/alternative harmony

Even in the DAW, rock benefits from performance. Layer rhythm guitars L/R (double tracking). DI + amp sim or real

27. Metal (Modern & Substyles Overview)
Precision, weight, guitar tone science, extreme dynamics
Typical BPM
Wide: 60 doom to 220+ extreme; modern metalcore often dual feels
Feel
Palm-muted chugs, double kick, breakdown half-time, blast beats (extreme)
Keys
Minor, Phrygian, harmonic minor; djent often low-tuned riff modality
Core elements
High-gain guitars, tight DI bass, triggered/hybrid drums, aggressive vocals
Mix vibe
Guitar clarity with heavy low end; kick/guitar carve; vocal intelligibility in chaos
Guitar production
• Tuning: drop tunings and 7/8-string extended range common in modern styles.
• DI recording + amp sims (or reamp) for editability.
• Tight editing of chugs to grid for modern metal; more human for classic/thrash feels.
• Quad-track rhythms (double L/R) carefully phase-aligned.
• Lead tones: higher gain mid-focused, delay/verb for solos.
Drums & low end
Kick and guitar fight for 100–200 Hz — carve with EQ. Sample reinforcement on kick/snare is standard in modern
metal. Bass often follows guitar rhythm with high-mids for click on small speakers plus sub weight. Breakdowns:
half-time, open space, maximum impact hits.
Substyle pointers
• Thrash: speed, palm mutes, aggressive picks, less low-tuned than djent.
• Death/black: extreme vocals, blasts, darker production philosophies differ (raw vs modern polish).
• Metalcore/djent: polyrhythms, low chugs, electronic textures sometimes, huge choruses in metalcore.
• Doom: slow, massive sustained weight, darker spaces.

31. Latin: Reggaeton, Salsa, Bachata, Latin Trap

Reggaeton production
The dembow rhythm is the heartbeat — a specific kick/snare relationship derived from dancehall/reggae lineage and
popularized globally. Program authentic dembow first, then melody. Modern reggaeton blends with pop and EDM:

Latin trap

atmospheres with Latin percussion ornaments.

37. Experimental / Genre-Bending Strategy
How to invent fusions without random mess
Typical BPM
Whatever serves the idea
Feel
Defined by your hybrid contract
Keys
Free
Core elements
One clear “center of gravity” + borrowed ornaments
Mix vibe
Consistency of space and vocal/lead treatment glues hybrids
A method that works
1. Choose a primary genre contract (e.g., “this is a house track”).
2. Borrow ONE foreign element (e.g., trap hats, metal guitar, salsa piano).
3. Keep drums or bass in the primary genre so the body still recognizes the home style.
4. Unify with consistent mix bus processing and a recurring motif.
5. Name the experiment after the feeling, not after every influence.
Producer tip: Hybrids fail when every element is from a different genre at once. Hierarchy beats collage.

39. Plugin & Stock-Tool Cookbook
Universal stock-tool strategies (any DAW)
• EQ: high-pass junk; cut mud 200–400 Hz; presence 2–5 kHz for vocals; air 10–12 kHz sparingly.
• Compressor: 2:1–4:1 for vocals; faster for control, slower for transparency; parallel for punch.
• Saturator/clipper: harmonics for small speakers; clip drum bus carefully.
• Sidechain: kick triggers bus duck on bass/pads (house/EDM/pop).
• Stereo imager: widen only above ~150–200 Hz; keep sub mono.
• Delay: tempo sync 1/8, 1/4, dotted; high-pass/low-pass the wet signal.
• Reverb: short rooms for drums; longer halls for pads; pre-delay to keep vocals clear.
Genre-oriented tool choices (examples, not endorsements)
• Trap/hip-hop: good 808 (or Serum/Phase Plant/3xOSC design), tuner, vocal chain, drum sampler.
• EDM: wavetable synth, strong limiter, transient shaper, stereo tools, LFO modulators.

40. Finishing Checklist (Any Genre)
I Does the track announce its genre (or hybrid intent) within 15 seconds?
I Is there a clear focal point in every section (vocal, bass riff, lead, or drums)?
I Kick–bass relationship clean on club system / car / phone?
I Hooks memorable without the rest of the arrangement?
I Arrangement contrast: verse vs chorus OR break vs drop actually different?
I Automation rides: energy moves, not static loops for 3 minutes?
I Transitions designed (not abrupt accidental cuts unless aesthetic)?
I Vocal intelligibility (if present) on small speakers?
I Mono compatibility checked?
I Referenced against 2 commercial tracks at matched loudness?
I Exported with correct sample rate/bit depth; true peak headroom for streaming?
I Metadata, version notes, and dry/wet vocal stems archived for future edits?
Glossary (Selected)
808: Originally TR-808 drum machine; now usually means long tuned bass/kick sine used in hip-hop/trap.
Sidechain: Using one signal (often kick) to control compression/volume of another (often bass).
Reese: Detuned, moving bass sound common in DnB.
Supersaw: Many detuned saw oscillators stacked for huge trance/EDM leads.
Dembow: Foundational rhythmic pattern in reggaeton.
One-drop: Reggae drum feel emphasizing particular beats / de-emphasizing the one in classic forms.
Clave: Guiding rhythmic key pattern in Afro-Cuban music.
Half-time: Snare placement that makes a fast BPM feel half as fast (common in trap/dubstep).
Ghost note: Soft drum hit that creates groove between main accents.
Parallel compression: Blend heavily compressed signal under dry for punch without killing dynamics.
Headroom: Space before 0 dBFS; needed for processing and mastering.
Topline: Main vocal melody/lyric line in pop writing sessions.
Break / breakbeat: Drum loop isolated from older funk records; foundational to jungle/hip-hop history.
Sound selection: Choosing the right sample/preset before processing — often 80% of the result.

Genre is a shared language between maker and listener. Learn the language fluently enough that you can speak it
without an accent — then develop your own dialect. The producers who stand out are not those who ignore genre,
nor those who only copy surface presets, but those who understand the deep contracts (groove, space, bass
behavior, vocal role, arrangement energy) and then make one bold personal choice inside that frame.
Practice plan: pick one genre per week. Finish two short tracks (60–90 seconds is fine) using only that genre’s drum
identity and bass identity. Reference constantly. On week four, hybrid two of them using the hierarchy method in
Chapter 37. In three months you will hear music differently — as systems of decisions rather than magic you either
have or don’t.
This handbook is intentionally practical and broad. Each genre contains decades of history and regional micro-styles
that reward deeper study: listen widely, credit sources, clear samples when releasing commercially, and collaborate
with musicians who live inside traditions you are still learning.
