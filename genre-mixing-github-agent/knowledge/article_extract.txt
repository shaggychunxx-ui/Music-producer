## Thesis
Genres differ not only in musical content but in production quality and convention. Tailor recording and mixing to genre intent (e.g. pure folk vocal vs club-ready drum & bass).

### Contrasting examples (intro)
- Folk singer + acoustic: preserve purity, sparse mix, wide dynamic range, possibly room noise for atmosphere; home listening context.
- Drum & bass + warped acapella: heavy compression, careful EQ, sound-system impact at club volume.

Same tools (EQ, etc.) can produce opposite aesthetics depending on intent.

---

## What Are Recording and Mixing Techniques?

### Recording
Everything before a sound file exists in the DAW:
- Amp choice (e.g. rock guitar amp shapes tone)
- Microphone selection (condenser for detail/modern; ribbon for warm/natural jazz interplay)
- Capture of performance and space

### Mixing
After recording, usually in a DAW: balance and refine elements into a cohesive track.
Core toolkit: EQ, compression, FX, panning — each used differently by genre.

## Core Mixing Tools

### EQ — Shaping & Sculpting
- Boost/cut frequency ranges
- Cut low end to reduce mud; boost mids for vocal presence
- Genre weight differs:
  - Pop: bright, polished; boosted highs; controlled mids; clarity and punch
  - Metal: scalpel carving for distorted guitars + aggressive drums; emphasize low mids and highs for intensity
- Always ask: what should come forward? what feeling?

### Compression — Punch & Tightness
- Reduces dynamic range above threshold; tracks, buses, or mix bus
- Hip-hop / electronic: heavier compression for punchy kick/bass
- Jazz / classical: subtle or none to keep natural dynamics
- Settings (attack, release, ratio) are genre- and source-specific
- Heavy compression → aggressive sound; over-compress → dull/squashed

### FX — Depth & Texture
- Reverb: space (room to hall)
- Chorus: width/richness
- Saturation: harmonic warmth (subtler than distortion)
- Shoegaze: heavy reverb + chorus (ethereal wash)
- Classical: minimal FX; natural room acoustics
- Genre-defining pedals/plugins common

### Panning — Space & Sensation
- Stereo placement to reduce clashes
- Traditional L/R vs modern mid-side (M/S) control of width/focus
- Orchestral: stage-realistic positions
- Electronic: creative movement across field

## Rock / Indie

Evolution: borrowed polish from pop while keeping impact.

- **Distortion**: cornerstone on guitars; The Strokes even distorted Casablancas’ vocal mic for Is This It gritty lo-fi
- **Layer guitars**: re-record riffs with different FX/pan for depth
- **Parallel / New York compression**: blend heavily compressed + dry (esp. drums) for punch without killing dynamics (e.g. Chris Lord-Alge)
- **Analogue character**: Neve 1073, UA 1176 favorites; saturation/warmth
- **Reverb on guitars**: plate (bright/metallic) to hall (ambient)

---

## Pop / Hip-hop / R&B

Polished, high-production studio craft.

- **Autotune**: creative signature (Billie Eilish, Travis Scott, etc.)
- **Harmonies**: quieter, heavily processed layers for lushness
- **Surgical EQ**: carve harshness; vocal clarity (e.g. Max Martin approach)
- **Sampling**: foundational hip-hop craft (e.g. Kanye-style transform of records)
- **808s**: Roland-lineage low-end pulse across beat-driven pop/hip-hop/R&B (Marvin Gaye → Drake lineage cited)

---

## Dance Music

Designed for large sound systems; clarity and impact at high volume.

- **Mix check in mono**: clubs often mono; solidify kick/bass; avoid phase collapse
- **Split bass**: sub-bass + mid-bass for tight/phat low end without mud
- **Sidechain compression**: duck bass/other elements to kick → pumping (e.g. Actress “Lost” ghost-kick idea)
- **Automation**: especially melodic elements and drops; signature plugin chains

---

## Jazz / Folk / Classical / Experimental

Showcase performance and instrument mastery; capture natural acoustic environment.

- **Room acoustics + mic placement**: critical (e.g. Al Schmitt jazz/classical approach)
- **Minimal processing**: spare compression/EQ; preserve dynamics (classical emotional range)
- Experimental: maximal processing often **pre-recording**; avoid “fixing” in the mix
- **Reverb**: natural halls (convolution/Altiverb-class tools)
- **Ambient samples**: immersive soundscape layer (experimental)

## Bottom-up vs Top-down Mixing

### Bottom-up
- Start with individual tracks (kick, bass, lead vocal…)
- Precise per-element balance; good for complex multi-part productions
- Avoid later conflicts by detailing early

### Top-down
- Process whole mix first (bus compressors, mix EQ, saturation)
- Establish vibe/tonal balance early
- Common in pop/electronic for cohesive sound quickly
- Can reduce over-processing of individuals
- Pros often keep signature master-bus chains as templates

---

## Closing philosophy
- No absolute right/wrong; justify technique against intent
- Develop personal signature notes (e.g. “chorus on Y, pan hats L/R, compress after A if B…”)
- Keep learning new plugins/tools including AI production tools
